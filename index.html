About

Sarawak

Sarawak, commonly known as 'The Land of Hornbills’ is located northwest on the untamed island of Borneo. Strong primeval forces and tribal traditions can still be found in every corner of Sarawak, from longhouses to the majestic highlands, from nostalgic city alleyways to vibrant rivers that run through the lavish splurge of rainforest canopies. Sarawak is a wildlife refuge and home to 27 ethnic groups, each with its own language and culture.

Topography


Sarawak is split into three regions: coastal lowlands with peat swamps and narrow deltaic and alluvial plains, a huge expanse of undulating hills up to 300 meters, and mountain highlands that reach to the Kalimantan border. It is home to Malaysia's longest river, the Batang Rajang, which runs from the Iran Mountain range southwest to Kapit, where it swings westward to finish its 563-kilometer journey to the South China Sea.Ocean-going vessels may navigate the river for 130 kilometres to Sibu.

UNESCO World Heritage Site


Gunung Mulu National Park Sarawak also has a UNESCO World Heritage Site: Gunung Mulu National Park. Mulu is the world's most researched tropical karst region, known for its rich biodiversity and karst characteristics. The Sarawak Chamber, measuring 600 metres by 415 metres and 80 metres high, is the world's biggest known cave chamber.

PEOPLE and CULTURES


The Land of Hornbills offers a unique experience rich in culture and natural splendor. It is brimming with cultural variety, history, huge rivers, ancient jungles, and natural wonders. Sarawakians follow a number of religions, including Islam, Christianity, Chinese folk religion, and animism. Christianity is the most popular religion in Sarawak, which is both culturally and religiously diverse. Religion has an important part in developing a culture of decency and humility among Sarawakians. It also reflects and reinforces the identities of diverse ethnic groups.
